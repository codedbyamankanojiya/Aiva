CYBERSECURITY 

Q1: Identifying and handling a security breach

Security breach

· An unauthorized person gets into your systems or data.
· A breach can happen through hacking, lost devices, stolen passwords, or physical break-ins.
· The longer a breach stays hidden, the more damage it causes.
· Not every breach leads to data theft – sometimes just viewing data counts.

Incident response

· A structured plan for what to do when a breach happens.
· Everyone needs to know their role before an attack occurs.
· A good plan reduces damage, recovery time, and legal trouble.
· Without a plan, people panic and make mistakes.

Threat detection

· Finding signs that an attack is happening or has already happened.
· Tools include antivirus, intrusion detection systems, and SIEM (security information event management).
· Employees reporting strange things (slow computers, weird pop-ups) also helps with detection.
· Modern detection looks for unusual behavior, not just known viruses.

Containment

· Stopping the breach from spreading to other parts of the network.
· Short-term containment might mean disconnecting one computer from the internet.
· Long-term containment might mean taking an entire server offline.
· Act fast but carefully – do not destroy evidence needed for investigation.

Investigation

· Finding out how the breach happened, what was accessed, and who did it.
· Investigators check log files, system records, network traffic, and deleted files.
· The investigation decides if you must notify customers, regulators, or police.
· Finding the root cause prevents the same breach from happening again.

Security protocols

· Step-by-step procedures to follow during and after a breach.
· Examples: who to call first, when to shut down systems, how to save evidence.
· Protocols should be written down and tested regularly.
· Keep protocols accessible even when your main systems are down.

Emergency procedures

· Immediate actions taken in the first minutes after discovering a breach.
· Steps include activating the response team, notifying leaders, and disconnecting affected systems.
· Procedures must be simple enough to follow when people are scared or stressed.
· Regular drills help everyone remember what to do without reading a manual during a real attack.

Q2: Educating employees about security best practices

Security awareness

· Employees understand that security is everyone's job, not just IT's job.
· Aware employees think twice before clicking links, plugging in USB drives, or sharing passwords.
· Awareness is about building good habits, not creating fear.
· The best security tools fail if one employee accidentally lets an attacker in.

Employee training

· Teaching specific security skills like creating strong passwords and spotting fake emails.
· Training happens when employees join and then every few months as a refresher.
· One training session per year is not enough – people forget within weeks.
· Good training uses real examples from your own company.

Security best practices

· Simple, proven rules that reduce risk without making work too difficult.
· Examples: lock your screen when leaving your desk, do not write down passwords, report suspicious emails.
· Best practices should be written in simple language and posted where employees see them daily.
· If a practice makes work too hard, employees will find ways around it – so balance security with convenience.

Phishing prevention

· Phishing is fake emails pretending to be from trusted companies to steal passwords or money.
· Teach employees to check the sender's email address, not just the display name.
· Hover over links before clicking to see where the link actually goes.
· Send fake phishing tests to train employees in a safe environment.

Security culture

· The shared belief that protecting data is important and valued by everyone.
· In a strong culture, employees remind each other about security without feeling annoying.
· Leaders must model good behavior – if the CEO reuses passwords, no one else will care.
· Building culture takes years but turns employees into your first line of defense.

Compliance education

· Teaching employees about specific laws and regulations that apply to your industry.
· Examples: healthcare workers learn HIPAA, finance workers learn data protection laws.
· Compliance training is often required by law, and companies can be fined if employees are not trained.
· Good training helps employees understand why rules exist and how to follow them in real work.

Q3: Implementing basic network security

Network security

· Protecting data as it travels between computers, servers, and the internet.
· Includes hardware (firewalls, routers) and software (antivirus, intrusion detection).
· The goal is to block bad traffic while letting good traffic flow normally.
· Network security is like walls, locks, and security cameras for your digital office.

Firewall setup

· A device or software that filters traffic coming into and leaving your network.
· Works like a security guard checking IDs – only allowed traffic gets through.
· Basic setup means blocking all unnecessary ports and allowing only essential services like email and web browsing.
· Firewalls need regular updates because new threats appear daily.

Network segmentation

· Splitting your network into smaller, separate sections like rooms in a house.
· If an attacker breaks into one section (like guest WiFi), they cannot reach sensitive data sections.
· Segmentation can be physical (separate cables) or virtual (using software rules).
· A simple example: keep guest WiFi completely separate from your cash register network.

Access control

· Deciding who can see what data and what they can do with it.
· The principle of least privilege means giving employees only the access they need for their job – nothing more.
· A receptionist does not need access to payroll files.
· Access should be reviewed regularly because employees change roles over time.

Security monitoring

· Constantly watching network traffic, login attempts, and system logs for signs of trouble.
· Monitoring can be done by people (security analysts) or software (SIEM systems).
· Without monitoring, you only discover a breach when something breaks or a customer complains – often too late.
· Basic monitoring starts with reviewing firewall logs daily and setting up alerts for suspicious activity.

Basic security infrastructure

· Essential tools every small company needs: firewall, antivirus, backup system, and log management.
· Also includes documented procedures for patching software and changing default passwords.
· Infrastructure does not have to be expensive – many free open source tools work well for small budgets.
· The most important part is keeping it maintained – an outdated firewall is almost as bad as no firewall.

Q4: Staying updated with the latest security threats

Security awareness

· Knowing that threats change constantly and you must keep learning.
· What was safe last year may be dangerous today because hackers found new weaknesses.
· Being aware means you never assume you know everything.
· Stay humble and curious about new attacks.

Threat intelligence

· Information about who is attacking, what methods they use, and what they are after.
· Intelligence comes from security companies, government alerts, and industry groups.
· Good intelligence helps you prioritize – if no one is attacking your industry with a certain method, focus elsewhere.
· Intelligence is different from news – news tells you something happened; intelligence tells you if it affects you.

Security news

· Reports of new data breaches, newly discovered vulnerabilities, and new laws.
· Reliable sources: KrebsOnSecurity, The Hacker News, BleepingComputer, and official CVE listings.
· Scan headlines daily and read deeply only on topics relevant to your job.
· Following news helps you learn from other companies' mistakes.

Industry updates

· Changes specific to your field – new rules for banks, new standards for hospitals, new requirements for government contractors.
· Different industries face different threats – retailers worry about payment card theft, law firms worry about client data leaks.
· Joining industry-specific security groups gives you access to alerts that only affect your type of business.
· Updates also include new versions of the software and hardware you actually use.

Continuous learning

· Spending a little time every week learning something new about security.
· Listen to podcasts during your commute, watch YouTube tutorials, or take free online courses.
· Certifications like Security+ or CISSP force structured learning, but you can also learn informally.
· The best professionals treat learning as a daily habit, not a once-a-year event.

Security research

· Going beyond news to read actual technical reports, white papers, and academic papers.
· Researchers publish detailed analyses of new attack methods, often with code examples.
· Following researchers on social media gives you early access to findings before they become mainstream news.
· You do not need to do original research yourself – reading others' research is enough.

INTERMEDIATE 

Q1: Designing a comprehensive security architecture

Security architecture

· The blueprint of how all security tools, policies, and people work together.
· Includes diagrams showing where firewalls sit, how data flows, and who has access to what.
· Good architecture is planned before buying tools – not buying tools first and figuring out where to put them later.
· Architecture balances security with business needs – too much security breaks the business, too little breaks security.

Comprehensive security

· Covering all areas: network, endpoints, data, applications, physical security, and people.
· Does not mean buying every tool – it means making sure there are no obvious gaps attackers can exploit.
· A comprehensive approach includes backups, disaster recovery, and incident response, not just prevention.
· You cannot be 100% secure, but comprehensive security makes you too hard for most attackers to bother with.

Defense in depth

· Using multiple layers of security so if one layer fails, another layer catches the attack.
· Example: firewall blocks some attacks, antivirus catches others, employee training stops the rest.
· No single security tool is perfect – layers make up for each other's weaknesses.
· Also applies to processes: have backups of backups and multiple ways to detect attacks.

Security controls

· Specific tools or actions that reduce a specific risk.
· Controls can be technical (firewalls, encryption), administrative (policies, training), or physical (locks, cameras).
· Controls are often described as preventive (stop attacks), detective (find attacks), or corrective (fix damage after an attack).
· Choosing controls means balancing cost, effectiveness, and ease of use – expensive controls that nobody uses are worthless.

Security frameworks

· Structured guides that tell you what to do without telling you exactly how to do it.
· Examples: NIST Cybersecurity Framework, ISO 27001, CIS Controls, and COBIT.
· Frameworks help you build a complete program rather than just buying random tools.
· Many frameworks are free and include maturity models that help you improve over years.

System security design

· Building security into new systems from the start, not adding it later as an afterthought.
· Includes designing databases to encrypt sensitive fields and applications to validate user input.
· Also means planning for failure – what happens if this server crashes or this user account is stolen?
· Adding security early in the design phase is cheaper and more effective than fixing problems later.

Q2: Handling security compliance requirements like GDPR

Security compliance

· Following the laws, regulations, and standards that apply to your industry and location.
· Being compliant does not mean being secure – you can check all boxes and still get hacked.
· Non-compliance can lead to huge fines, lawsuits, and losing the right to do business.
· Compliance is the minimum standard, not the goal – good security goes beyond what the law requires.

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

· A European law that protects the personal data of EU citizens, no matter where the company is located.
· Requires getting permission before collecting data, deleting data when asked, and reporting breaches within 72 hours.
· Fines can reach 20 million euros or 4% of global annual revenue – whichever is larger.
· Companies outside Europe must follow GDPR if they have any EU customers or process EU citizen data.

Regulatory requirements

· Specific rules written by government agencies that you must follow by law.
· Examples: HIPAA for healthcare, PCI DSS for credit card companies, SOX for public companies.
· Different countries have different rules – operating in multiple countries means following all of them.
· Requirements change over time, so compliance is an ongoing process, not a one-time project.

Compliance frameworks

· Structured guides that help you meet regulatory requirements in an organized way.
· Examples: SOC 2 for service providers, FedRAMP for US government cloud services, ISO 27701 for privacy management.
· Frameworks provide checklists and control lists that auditors will test against.
· Using a recognized framework makes compliance easier because the work has already been mapped out for you.

Audit preparation

· Getting all your documentation, evidence, and processes ready before an auditor arrives.
· Includes having written policies, access logs, training records, and incident reports.
· Auditors will test whether you actually do what your policies say – not just whether you have policies.
· Regular internal audits (self-checks) help you find and fix problems before the real audit.

Documentation requirements

· Keeping written records of everything related to compliance: policies, procedures, risk assessments, and incident responses.
· Documentation proves to auditors and regulators that you are following the rules.
· Bad documentation is almost as bad as non-compliance – if you cannot prove you did something, an auditor assumes you did not.
· Documentation should be stored securely but also be accessible when needed for audits or legal requests.

Q3: Conducting security assessments and penetration testing

Security assessment

· A general review of your security posture to find weaknesses and gaps.
· Includes reviewing policies, checking configurations, interviewing staff, and scanning for known vulnerabilities.
· Assessments can be done internally by your own team or externally by consultants.
· The output is a report of findings with recommendations for fixing each issue.

Penetration testing (pen test)

· An authorized simulated attack on your systems to see if real attackers could break in.
· Testers use the same tools and methods as real hackers but stop before causing actual damage.
· A pen test shows what is actually exploitable, not just what looks weak on paper.
· After the test, you get a report showing exactly how they got in and how to fix it.

Vulnerability assessment

· Using automated tools to scan your systems for known security holes (missing patches, weak configurations, default passwords).
· Less expensive and faster than a full penetration test.
· Produces a long list of potential issues, but not all of them are actually exploitable.
· Should be run regularly (weekly or monthly) because new vulnerabilities are discovered constantly.

Security testing

· The broader category that includes vulnerability scans, pen tests, and security assessments.
· Also includes specific tests like wireless network testing, physical security testing, and social engineering tests (trying to trick employees).
· Different tests find different types of problems – no single test finds everything.
· Testing should happen on a schedule (quarterly or yearly) and also after any major system change.

Risk analysis

· Evaluating each identified vulnerability based on how likely it is to be exploited and how much damage it would cause.
· A low-risk issue might be a minor problem on an unimportant server.
· A high-risk issue might be an admin password on a public-facing server containing customer data.
· Risk analysis helps you prioritize – fix the high-risk issues first, low-risk issues can wait.

Security validation

· Continuously testing whether your security controls are actually working as expected.
· Different from one-time assessments – validation happens on an ongoing basis (daily, weekly, monthly).
· Can be automated using breach and attack simulation (BAS) tools that run safe tests automatically.
· Validation answers the question: "If an attacker tried this method right now, would we stop them?"

Q4: Implementing incident response procedures

Incident response

· The structured process for handling a security incident from detection to recovery.
· Includes specific roles (who does what), communication plans (who to tell), and technical steps (how to contain and remove threats).
· A good incident response plan reduces damage, recovery time, and legal liability.
· Without a plan, people panic, evidence gets destroyed, and the same attack can happen again.

Security procedures

· Step-by-step written instructions for specific types of incidents.
· Examples: what to do for a ransomware infection, what to do for a stolen laptop, what to do for a phishing attack that succeeded.
· Procedures should be short enough to follow during a stressful incident – one or two pages maximum.
· Procedures must be tested through drills and tabletop exercises (walking through a fake scenario with the team).

Response planning

· Preparing for incidents before they happen, not scrambling during an attack.
· Includes identifying critical systems (what must be protected first), backup systems (how to restore data), and communication trees (who calls whom).
· Also includes pre-approval for certain actions – for example, "the security lead can disconnect any system without asking permission during an active breach."
· Response planning should be reviewed and updated every six months because systems and threats change.

Communication protocols

· Rules about who talks to whom during and after an incident.
· Includes internal communication (IT team, executives, legal, PR) and external communication (customers, regulators, law enforcement, media).
· Bad communication can make an incident worse – for example, telling customers too early (before knowing facts) or too late (after damage is done).
· Communication protocols should include templates for common messages so you are not writing from scratch during a crisis.

Forensic analysis

· Collecting and examining digital evidence to understand exactly what happened during an incident.
· Includes making exact copies of hard drives (forensic images), analyzing log files, recovering deleted files, and tracking attacker movements.
· Proper forensics preserves evidence so it can be used in court if needed.
· Forensics must follow strict procedures – if done wrong, evidence can be thrown out of court or you might never know the full story.

Recovery procedures

· The steps to get systems back to normal operation after an incident is contained.
· Includes restoring data from clean backups, rebuilding compromised servers, resetting all passwords, and patching the vulnerability that allowed the attack.
· Recovery is not finished until you have confirmed that the attacker cannot immediately get back in the same way.
· After recovery, a post-incident review (lessons learned) should happen to improve future response.

ADVANCED LEVEL

Q1: Building a security operations center (SOC)

SOC creation

· A dedicated team and facility that monitors for security threats 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.
· You can build your own SOC (expensive but full control) or hire a managed service (cheaper but less control).
· SOCs run in shifts because attacks do not only happen during business hours.
· Building a SOC takes months or years and requires budget, skilled people, and the right technology.

Security operations

· The daily work of the SOC – watching alerts, investigating suspicious activity, and responding to incidents.
· Includes triage (sorting real threats from false alarms) and escalation (sending serious issues to senior analysts).
· Security operations also include proactive hunting for threats that automated systems might miss.
· Operations run on playbooks – pre-written step-by-step guides for handling common alert types.

Monitoring systems

· Tools the SOC uses to watch for threats – SIEM (collects and analyzes logs), EDR (watches laptops and servers), and NDR (watches network traffic).
· These tools generate thousands of alerts per day, but most are false alarms or low priority.
· Good monitoring systems use rules and machine learning to filter out noise and highlight real threats.
· Monitoring systems must be tuned regularly – too sensitive and analysts get overwhelmed, not sensitive enough and real attacks are missed.

Threat detection

· Finding attacks that have bypassed firewalls, antivirus, and other basic security tools.
· Detection can be signature-based (looking for known attack patterns), behavior-based (spotting unusual activity), or anomaly-based (finding things that deviate from normal).
· Modern SOCs use threat intelligence to know what attackers are currently doing and look for those specific behaviors.
· Detection is a constant cat-and-mouse game – attackers change their methods as soon as defenders learn to detect the old methods.

Incident response

· The SOC is often the first team to detect an incident and the team that leads the response.
· SOC analysts contain threats (isolating infected systems), remove them (deleting malware), and recover systems (restoring from clean backups).
· For large incidents, the SOC works with legal, PR, executives, and sometimes law enforcement.
· The SOC also conducts post-incident reviews to figure out how to prevent similar incidents in the future.

Security team building

· Hiring, training, and keeping skilled security analysts who can work well under pressure.
· SOC roles include tier 1 (alert triage), tier 2 (incident investigation), tier 3 (advanced threat hunting), and SOC manager (oversight and reporting).
· Security talent is rare and expensive – keeping people requires good pay, career growth, and preventing burnout.
· Team building also includes cross-training so multiple people can handle any role if someone is sick or leaves.

Q2: Implementing zero-trust security architecture

Zero trust

· A security model that assumes no one is trusted by default – not inside your network and not outside.
· Old model: trust everything inside the corporate network. Zero trust: verify everyone and everything, every single time.
· The core principle is "never trust, always verify."
· Zero trust assumes attackers are already inside your network, so you must protect every resource individually.

Security architecture

· Zero trust requires redesigning how users, devices, and applications connect to each other.
· Instead of a strong outer wall (firewall) and weak inside, zero trust puts small walls around every single resource.
· Every access request is treated as if it comes from an untrusted network, even if it comes from inside your office.
· Architecture includes micro-segmentation (splitting the network into tiny pieces) and per-application access policies.

Identity verification

· Verifying who the user is before granting any access – not just a password but also other factors.
· Multi-factor authentication (MFA) is required for every login – password plus phone code or fingerprint.
· Identity verification happens continuously, not just at login – if behavior changes (logging in from a new country at 3 AM), re-verify or block.
· Identity is the new perimeter – instead of protecting the network, zero trust protects user identities.

Continuous monitoring

· Watching user and device behavior constantly, not just checking once at login.
· If a user normally accesses 10 files per day but suddenly downloads 10,000 files, block and alert.
· Continuous monitoring uses analytics to establish normal baselines for each user and device.
· When behavior deviates from normal, access can be reduced (read-only instead of edit) or blocked entirely.

Network security

· In zero trust, the network is considered hostile, even the internal corporate network.
· All traffic is encrypted, even inside your data center – never assume internal traffic is safe.
· Micro-segmentation breaks the network into tiny zones – a breach in one zone cannot spread to others.
· Network security becomes about controlling connections between individual workloads, not just blocking traffic at the border.

Modern security

· Zero trust is designed for modern work patterns – cloud apps, remote workers, personal devices, and contractors.
· Old perimeter security fails when employees work from coffee shops and use SaaS applications.
· Zero trust works everywhere because it does not rely on being inside a physical office.
· Modern security also means automating responses – when a threat is detected, access is cut off automatically without waiting for a human.

Q3: Handling advanced persistent threats (APT)

APT handling

· APTs are long-term, targeted attacks by skilled attackers – often nation-states or organized criminal groups.
· Unlike random attacks, APTs spend months inside your network, moving slowly and carefully to avoid detection.
· Handling APTs requires advanced skills, specialized tools, and significant time – this is not a quick cleanup.
· APT groups target specific organizations for espionage, data theft, or sabotage – not random victims.

Advanced threats

· Threats that use custom malware, unknown vulnerabilities (zero-days), and clever social engineering.
· Advanced threats bypass traditional security tools like antivirus and firewalls.
· Attackers study your defenses before attacking and change their methods to evade detection.
· Advanced threats often use "living off the land" – using legitimate system tools like PowerShell instead of installing new malware.

Threat hunting

· Proactively searching for signs of APTs that automated detection systems might miss.
· Hunters look for subtle indicators – unusual login times, small data transfers, weird process executions, abnormal network connections.
· Threat hunting starts with a hypothesis ("an attacker might be using this technique") and then searches for evidence.
· Hunting is different from monitoring – monitoring reacts to alerts, hunting looks for what did not trigger alerts.

Incident response for APTs

· APT incident response is long and complex – months of investigation and cleanup, not days.
· Standard incident response (contain, remove, recover) is not enough – APTs often leave backdoors for re-entry.
· Response includes full network rebuild (reimaging every system), resetting every password, and continuous monitoring for months after cleanup.
· Many organizations hire outside incident response firms to handle APTs because internal teams lack the skills or time.

Security monitoring for APTs

· APT monitoring requires collecting and keeping more data than usual – months or years of logs, not just weeks.
· Monitoring must cover unusual places – DNS logs, proxy logs, email metadata, and endpoint process creation.
· Correlation across different data sources is critical – a suspicious login alone might be nothing, but combined with unusual network traffic becomes a lead.
· Monitoring for APTs also means watching for signs of data staging (collecting files before stealing them) and command and control traffic (attackers checking in with their malware).

Advanced defense

· Defending against APTs requires multiple layers – next-gen antivirus, endpoint detection and response (EDR), network traffic analysis, and deception technology (fake systems that trap attackers).
· Advanced defense also means reducing the attack surface – removing unnecessary software, restricting admin rights, and hardening configurations.
· User training is still important – many APTs start with a single phishing email to one employee.
· No defense is perfect against APTs, so detection and response capabilities are just as important as prevention.

Q4: Designing security for cloud and multi-cloud environments

Cloud security

· Protecting data, applications, and infrastructure hosted in cloud providers like AWS, Azure, or Google Cloud.
· Cloud security follows the shared responsibility model – cloud provider secures the data center and hypervisor, customer secures their data, access, and configurations.
· Many cloud breaches happen because customers misconfigure settings – leaving storage buckets public, using weak passwords, or not enabling encryption.
· Cloud security requires new tools and skills – traditional on-premise firewalls and scanners do not work in the cloud.

Multi-cloud security

· Using multiple cloud providers at the same time (AWS plus Azure plus Google Cloud) to avoid relying on just one company.
· Security becomes more complex because each provider has different interfaces, permission models, and security tools.
· Multi-cloud requires consistent policies across all providers – the same access rules, encryption standards, and logging requirements everywhere.
· Centralized visibility is critical – you need one dashboard to see security status across all your cloud providers together.

Cloud security posture

· The overall security health of your cloud environment – how well you follow cloud security best practices.
· Cloud security posture management (CSPM) tools automatically scan for misconfigurations – open storage, overly permissive roles, unencrypted databases.
· Posture includes compliance checks – does your cloud environment meet PCI, HIPAA, or GDPR requirements?
· Good posture is never finished – as you add new cloud resources, you must continuously check and fix configurations.

Cloud compliance

· Meeting regulatory requirements when your data is stored in the cloud, not on your own servers.
· Major cloud providers have compliance certifications (SOC 2, ISO 27001, PCI DSS, HIPAA) – but that only covers the provider's part of security.
· Your configurations on top of the provider's platform must also be compliant – the provider's certification does not automatically make you compliant.
· Compliance in the cloud includes data residency (where data is physically stored) and audit trails (logs of who accessed what data and when).

Cloud security architecture

· Designing cloud systems securely from the start, not adding security after everything is already built.
· Includes network design (virtual private clouds, subnets, security groups), identity management (least privilege roles, temporary credentials), and data protection (encryption at rest and in transit).
· Architecture must account for auto-scaling – security controls should automatically apply to new servers as they spin up.
· Good cloud architecture uses infrastructure as code – security rules written as code and deployed automatically, not configured manually by hand.

Cloud security operations

· The daily work of monitoring and responding to threats in cloud environments.
· Includes cloud-native monitoring tools (AWS GuardDuty, Azure Security Center, Google Security Command Center) and third-party cloud security platforms.
· Operations must handle ephemeral resources – servers that exist for only minutes or hours – traditional monitoring assumes long-lived servers.
· Cloud security operations also include automated remediation – when a misconfiguration is found, fix it automatically without waiting for a human.
