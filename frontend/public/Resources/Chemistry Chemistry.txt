BASIC NOTES 

1.  pH Scale & Acid-Base Basics

•pH Scale Basics:

•Range: 0 to 14 (measured on logarithmic scale)

•pH < 7 = Acidic (more H+ ions than OH-ions)

•pH = 7 = Neutral (equal H+ and OH-ions)

•pH > 7 = Basic/Alkaline (more OH-ions than H+ ions)

•Each pH unit = 10x change in H+ concentration (logarithmic nature)

∆ Calculating pH:

•pH = -log[H+] (negative log of hydrogen ion concentration)

•Strong acids (like HCI, HNO3) completely dissociate in water

•Example: 0.01M HCI → [H+] = 0.01 M (complete dissociation)

pH = -log(0.01) = 2 (moderately acidic solution)

✓Quick Tips:

•Lower pH = more acidic = more H+ ions present

•Higher pH = more basic = fewer H+ ions, more OH- ions

•pH 0 = strong acid (like battery acid), pH 14 = strong base (like lye)


Avogadro's Number & Moles

2.  What is Avogadro's Number?

•Value: 6.022 × 1023 particles (atoms, molecules, ions)

•This many particles in 1 mole of any substance

•Like "dozen" (12) but for chemistry -much larger counting unit

∆Mole Concept:

•1 mole = 6.022 × 1023 particles (definition)

•Molar mass = mass of 1 mole in grams (numerically equal to atomic/molecular weight)

•Same number of particles: 12g C, 23g Na, 35.5g Cl

•Atoms are tiny - need this huge number to have measurable amounts

✓Why It Matters:

•Converts between mass (measurable) and number of particles (countable)

•Used in: Stoichiometry, gas laws, chemical equations, concentration

•Foundation of quantitative chemistry and laboratory work

Simple Example:

•1 mole H2O = 18g = 2H atoms + 10 atom = 6.022 × 1023 water molecules

2 moles H₂O = 36g = 12.044 × 1023 water molecules



3.  Hydrocarbons: Saturated vs Unsaturated

SATURATED Hydrocarbons (Alkanes):

•Formula: CnH2n+2 (where n = number of carbon atoms)

•Only single bonds between carbon atoms (C-C)

•Less reactive, chemically stable compounds

•Examples: Methane (CH4), Ethane (C2H6), Propane (C3H8)

Found in natural gas, petroleum fuels, waxes

UNSATURATED Hydrocarbons:

•Alkenes: CnH2n (one C=C double bond)

•Alkynes: CnH2n-2 (one C≡C triple bond)

•More reactive due to multiple bonds

•Undergo addition reactions (atoms add across double/triple bonds)

•Examples: Ethene (C2H4), Ethyne (C2H2)

4. Newton's Three Laws of Motion

#FIRST LAW - Law of Inertia:

•Object at rest stays at rest

•Object in motion continues in straight line at constant speed

•UNLESS acted upon by external unbalanced force

•Key idea: Things resist changes in motion (inertia)

Example: Book stays on table until someone applies force

#SECOND LAW - F = ma:

•Force = mass × acceleration(Newton)

•More force = more acceleration
(directly proportional)

•More mass = less acceleration
(inversely proportional)

•Direction: Force and acceleration in same direction

•Key idea: Quantifies exactly how force affects motion

Example: Pushing shopping cart harder = faster acceleration

#THIRD LAW - Action-Reaction:

•For every action, there's an equal and opposite reaction
•Forces always occur in pairs

•Action and reaction act on DIFFERENT objects

•Key idea: Forces are interactions between objects

•Example: Walking - you push ground backward, ground pushes you forward

✓Important Notes:

•These laws apply to objects moving at speeds much slower than light

•They don't work well for very small particles (quantum) or very fast objects (relativistic)

∆ Quick Reference Summary

pH Scale:

•pH = -log[H+] (negative logarithm of H+ concentration)
•Lower pH = more acidic = more H+ ions present
•pH 0-3 = strong acids, pH 7 = neutral, pH 11-14 = strong bases

Avogadro's Number:

•6.022 x 1023 particles = 1 mole
•Bridge between tiny atoms and measurable grams
•Essential for all chemical calculations

Hydrocarbons:
•Saturated = single bonds only, stable
•Unsaturated = double/triple bonds, reactive
•Addition reactions convert unsaturated to saturated

Newton's Laws:
1. Inertia (no change without external force)
2. F=ma (force causes acceleration)
3. Action-Reaction (forces always come in pairs)

Study Tip: Remember the relationships and formulas - they're the foundation for solving problems in chemistry and physics!

1 . Raoult's Law

What it is: Predicts how the vapor pressure of liquids changes when mixed together.

Why it matters: Explains why saltwater boils at higher temperatures and helps separate alcohol from water through distillation.

How it works: When you mix two liquids, the pressure above the mixture equals the weighted average of each pure liquid's pressure based on their proportions.

2. Markovnikov's Rule
What it is: Predicts where chemicals add to double bonds in organic reactions.

Why it matters: Determines which product forms when making plastics, pharmaceuticals, and many other chemicals.

How it works: In unsymmetrical alkenes, hydrogen adds to the carbon with more hydrogens, and the other chemical adds to the carbon with fewer hydrogens following stability rules.

Rule Statement:

•In addition of HX to unsymmetrical alkenes, H adds to carbon with more H atoms
•X adds to carbon with fewer H atoms

Reaction Mechanism:

1. Electrophilic attack: π-bond attacks H+ from HX

•Forms more stable carbocation intermediate

•Carbocation stability: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl

2. Carbocation formation:

•CH2=CH2 + HCI → CH3-CH2+ (primary)

•CH3-CH=CH2 + HCI → CH3-CH+-CH3 (secondary -favored)

3. Nucleophilic attack: Cl- attacks carbocation

CH3-CH+-CH3 + CI-→ CH3-CHCI-CH3 (2-chloropropane)

Examples:
•HBr + propene → 2-bromopropane (not 1-bromopropane)
•HCI + isobutene → tert-butyl chloride

Key Points:
•Regioselective addition
•Follows carbocation stability rule
•Applies to asymmetric alkenes
•Exceptions: Peroxides (anti-Markovnikov), borane addition

3. Capacitor

What it is: A device that stores electrical energy like a tiny battery but charges and discharges instantly.

Why it matters: Essential for smoothing power in electronics, timing circuits, and energy storage.

How it works: Two metal plates separated by an insulator accumulate opposite charges, creating an electric field that stores energy. The more area and closer the plates, the more energy it stores.

Capacitor Working

Basic Structure:
•Two conductive plates separated by dielectric
•Plates store equal and opposite charges
•Dielectric prevents charge flow between plates

Working Principle:
1. Charging process:
•Battery connects to plates
•Electrons accumulate on negative plate
•Positive charges induced on other plate
•Electric field E = V/d between plates

2. Charge storage:
•Q = CV (Charge = Capacitance × Voltage)
•C = ɛɛrA/d (Capacitance formula)
•Q = ne (where n = number of electrons)

3. Energy storage:
•U = 1/2CV2 = 1/2QV = 1/2(Q²/C)
•Energy stored in electric field
•U = 1280&r∫E2dV (volume integral)

Key Components:
•Plates: Conductive material (metal)
•Dielectric: Insulating material (air, ceramic, plastic)
•Dielectric constant ɛr: Relates to permittivity increase

Applications:
•Energy storage (camera flashes, computers)
•AC circuits (filters, timing)
•Power factor correction
•Signal coupling/decoupling

ADVANCED NOTES 

1. Reaction Kinetics & Rate Laws

Rate of Reaction:
•What it is: How fast reactants convert to products
•Measured as: Change in concentration per unit time
•Units: mol-L-1-s-1 or M-s-1

Rate Law Expression:
•Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
•Where k = rate constant, m,n reaction orders
•Overall order = m + n

Order of Reaction:
•Zeroth order: Rate = k (constant rate)
•First order: Rate = k[A] (rate x concentration)
•Second order: Rate = k[A]² (rate x concentration²)

Determination:
•Experimentally from concentration vs. time data
•From the exponents in the rate law
•Different from stoichiometric coefficients!

Important Points:
•Temperature affects rate constant (Arrhenius equation)
•Catalyst changes rate without being consumed
•Practical applications: Drug metabolism, industrial process control
•Experimental methods: Spectrophotometry, pressure measurements, conductivity

2.Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution

What it is: Replacing a hydrogen on benzene with another group through electrophile attack.

Why Benzene is Special:
•Stable ring with delocalized π-electrons
•Doesn't undergo addition reactions like alkenes
•Requires special mechanism to preserve aromaticity

EASA Mechanism Steps:
1. Generation of electrophile (E+)
2. π-complex formation (benzene attacks E+)
3. σ-complex formation (arenium ion intermediate)
4. Deprotonation (restores aromaticity)

Key Features:
•Resonance stabilization: Intermediate is stabilized by resonance
•Activation energy: Higher than typical addition reactions
•Regioselectivity: Position where substitution occurs

Common Examples:
•Nitration: C6H6 + NO2+ → C6H5NO2 + H+
•Halogenation: C6H6 + Br2 → C6H5Br + HBr
•Sulfonation: C6H6 + SO3 → C6H5SO3H

Electron-donating groups: Activate ring, direct ortho/para Electron-withdrawing groups: Deactivate ring, direct meta
•Industrial importance: Pharmaceuticals, dyes, explosives, polymers
