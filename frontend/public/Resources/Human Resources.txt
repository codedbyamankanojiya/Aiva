Part 1: Basics of Human Resources

What is Human Resources (HR)?

· HR means the people who work in an organization.
· People are the most important resource because they run everything else.
· Without people, machines, money, and buildings cannot do anything on their own.
· HR treats employees as assets, not just costs, because good employees make the business successful.

What is HR Management (HRM)?

· Managing people in a smart way to help the business succeed.
· Includes hiring, training, paying, and taking care of employees.
· HRM makes sure employees are happy, productive, and motivated to do their best work.
· It also helps the company save money by reducing turnover and avoiding legal problems.

How HR has changed over time:

· Old days: HR only did paperwork (payroll, forms, birthdays).
· Now: HR helps make business decisions and plan for the future.
· The old name was "Personnel Management" which sounded very administrative and boring.
· The new name "Human Resource Management" shows that people are valuable resources like money and materials.

Why HR changed:

· COVID-19 showed worker shortages.
· Technology grew fast.
· Companies now work globally.
· Employees today expect more than just a salary – they want purpose, flexibility, and growth.
· Social media made company culture visible to the public, so HR had to improve how employees are treated.

Main jobs of HR (5 functions):

1. Staffing – Hiring the right people.
2. Training – Teaching employees new skills.
3. Compensation – Paying salaries and benefits.
4. Employee relations – Handling disputes and keeping morale high.
5. Compliance – Following all labor laws.

· These five functions work together to support employees from the day they join until the day they leave.
· If any one function is weak (like bad training or unfair pay), the whole system suffers.

Part 2: Employee Lifecycle (What HR does step by step)

Recruitment and hiring:

· Goal: Put the right person in the right job.
· Wrong hire is expensive – so HR must be careful.
· A bad hire can cost the company many months of salary in lost time and productivity.
· HR uses interviews, tests, and reference checks to reduce the risk of hiring the wrong person.

Where to find candidates:

· Internal – promote or transfer current employees.
· External – job boards, campus hiring, social media, agencies.
· Internal hiring is cheaper and faster because the employee already knows the company culture.
· External hiring brings fresh ideas and new skills but takes more time and money.

Hiring steps:

1. Write job ad.
2. Screen applications.
3. Interview candidates.
4. Test skills if needed.
5. Check references.
6. Make job offer.
7. Reject other applicants politely.

· Each step removes unqualified candidates until only the best one remains.
· Skipping any step (like reference checks) can lead to hiring someone who looks good on paper but fails in real work.

Training vs. Development:

· Training – teaches skills for the current job (example: software training).
· Development – prepares for future jobs (example: leadership coaching).
· Training gives immediate results – the employee can do their current job better right away.
· Development takes longer but prepares the company for future leadership needs when senior people retire or leave.

How to keep employees from leaving (retention):

· Pay competitive salaries.
· Offer work-life balance (flexible hours, remote work).
· Recognize good work.
· Create a positive culture.
· Employees leave managers, not companies – so training managers to be good leaders is very important for retention.
· Regular feedback and career growth conversations make employees feel valued and less likely to search for other jobs.

Performance management (checking employee work):

· Set SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).
· Track progress regularly.
· Do formal reviews (yearly or quarterly).
· Reward good performance.
· Performance management is not just about punishing bad work – it is about helping good employees become even better.
· Without regular feedback, employees feel lost and may start working in the wrong direction without knowing it.

Part 3: Strategy, Culture, and Laws

What is human capital?

· The value of an employee's skills, knowledge, and experience.
· This is often a company's only unique advantage.
· Machines and buildings can be copied by competitors, but a skilled and motivated team cannot be easily replicated.
· Investing in human capital (training, good pay, good culture) gives higher returns than investing in equipment alone.

HR as a strategic partner:

· HR should not just do paperwork.
· HR should ask: "Do we have the right talent to grow the business?"
· When HR sits at the leadership table, the company makes better decisions about hiring, firing, and promotions.
· Without HR's input, leaders might make people decisions based on emotion or favoritism instead of data and fairness.

What is corporate culture?

· Shared values, beliefs, and behaviors of a company.
· Example: Some companies are formal; some are casual and fun.
· Culture is not written in any manual – it is "how things really get done around here."
· A strong culture attracts the right employees and pushes away the wrong ones naturally.

HR's role in culture:

· Writes the employee handbook (rules and values).
· Enforces code of conduct (example: no taking bribes from vendors).
· HR also investigates complaints about harassment, discrimination, or unethical behavior.
· When HR consistently enforces the rules for everyone (including senior leaders), trust in the culture grows.

Diversity and Inclusion (D&I):

· Diversity – having different types of people (race, gender, age).
· Inclusion – making sure everyone's voice is heard and valued.
· Diversity without inclusion is useless – you can hire diverse people but still make them feel unwelcome.
· Diverse teams make better decisions because they see problems from multiple angles, not just one narrow view.

Affirmative action:

· Actively recruiting women, minorities, and people with disabilities.
· Done to correct past discrimination.
· Affirmative action is not about hiring unqualified people – it is about finding qualified people from groups that were ignored in the past.
· Many countries have laws requiring affirmative action for government jobs and government contracts.

Important employment laws HR must follow:

· No discrimination based on race, religion, gender, age, or disability.
· Must pay minimum wage and overtime.
· Must provide a safe workplace (no hazards).
· Must allow medical or family leave.
· Must follow rules about unions and collective bargaining.
· Breaking these laws can lead to lawsuits, fines, and damage to the company's reputation.
· HR must stay updated on laws because they change frequently – what was legal last year may be illegal today.

Part 4: Key Terms and Modern Trends

Important vocabulary:

· Exempt employee – No overtime pay (usually managers).
· Non-exempt employee – Gets overtime pay (hourly workers).
· Progressive discipline – Warnings get stronger each time (verbal → written → suspension → fired).
· Turnover – Rate at which employees leave the company. High turnover is bad.
· Turnover is calculated as (number of leavers ÷ average total employees) × 100. High turnover means something is wrong.
· Progressive discipline gives employees a chance to correct their behavior before being fired, which also protects the company from wrongful termination lawsuits.

Modern trends in HR:

· Digital HR (HRIS) – Using software and AI to automate hiring, payroll, and data analysis.
· Employee well-being – Not just physical safety. Now includes mental health support.
· Remote and hybrid work – Managing teams that work from home and office.
· Learning organization – Company encourages continuous learning and upgrading skills.
· Digital HR saves time by automating repetitive tasks so HR staff can focus on strategic work like culture and employee relations.
· Remote work requires new skills like managing by output (results) instead of managing by watching people work at their desks.
