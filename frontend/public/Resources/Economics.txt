1: Demand and Supply

· Demand: Quantity of a good consumers are willing and able to buy at different prices. Law of demand – price ↑, demand ↓ (inverse relationship).
· Supply: Quantity producers are willing to sell at different prices. Law of supply – price ↑, supply ↑ (direct relationship).
· Market equilibrium: Point where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. No tendency to change.
· Price determination: Equilibrium price (market-clearing price) is determined by intersection of demand and supply curves.
· Market forces: Surplus (excess supply) pushes price down; shortage (excess demand) pushes price up.
· Elasticity: Measures responsiveness – elastic (small price change causes large quantity change) vs inelastic (small response).

---

2: GDP and Its Calculation

· GDP (Gross Domestic Product): Total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific time period (usually a year or quarter).
· Economic growth: Increase in GDP over time – primary indicator of economic development.
· Calculation methods (all yield same result):
  · Expenditure approach: GDP = C + I + G + (X - M) [Consumption + Investment + Government spending + Net exports]
  · Income approach: Sum of all incomes (wages, rent, interest, profit)
  · Production approach (value-added): Sum of value added at each production stage
· Per capita income: GDP divided by population – better measure of average living standards than total GDP.
· Economic indicators: Nominal GDP (current prices) vs Real GDP (inflation-adjusted) – real GDP shows true growth.

---

3: Types of Economic Systems

· Capitalism (market economy): Private ownership of resources, profit motive, price determined by supply/demand, minimal government intervention. Examples: USA, UK.
· Socialism (planned economy): Government/collective ownership of major resources, central planning, equitable distribution, price controls. Examples: China (current hybrid), Cuba.
· Mixed economy: Combines elements of capitalism and socialism – private sector with government regulation, social welfare programs, public goods. Examples: India, France, Canada.
· Economic models: Each system answers three basic questions – What to produce? How to produce? For whom to produce?
· Comparison: Capitalism excels at efficiency/innovation but can create inequality; Socialism ensures equity but may lack incentives; Mixed economy balances both.

---

 4: Inflation and Its Causes

· Inflation: Sustained increase in general price rise of goods and services over time (reduces purchasing power of money).
· Causes of inflation (two main types):
  · Demand-pull inflation: Aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply (too much money chasing too few goods). Causes – increased consumption, government spending, money supply growth.
  · Cost-push inflation: Production costs rise, pushing up prices. Causes – higher wages, raw material costs (e.g., oil), supply chain disruptions, taxes.
· Inflation control methods: Monetary policy (RBI raises interest rates, increases reserve ratios), fiscal policy (reduced government spending, higher taxes), supply-side measures.
· Economic impact: Hurts fixed-income groups, savers, lenders (real value erodes). Benefits borrowers, asset owners (real estate, gold).
· RBI policies: Inflation targeting (typically 2-6% range in India), repo rate adjustments, open market operations to control money supply.

INTERMEDIATE NOTES 

1: Monetary Policy and Its Tools

· Monetary policy: Actions taken by a central bank (RBI in India) to control money supply and credit in the economy to achieve economic stability and growth.

· RBI objectives: Price stability (control inflation), economic growth, exchange rate stability, employment generation.

· Monetary instruments (tools):
  · Repo rate: Rate at which RBI lends to commercial banks. Increasing repo rate reduces money supply (controls inflation).
  · Reverse repo rate: Rate RBI pays to banks for parking excess funds. Absorbs liquidity from market.
  · CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio) : Percentage of deposits banks must keep with RBI. Higher CRR reduces lending capacity.
  · SLR (Statutory Liquidity Ratio) : Percentage of deposits banks must invest in government securities.
  · Open Market Operations (OMO) : RBI buys/sells government securities to inject/absorb liquidity.
  · Bank Rate: Long-term lending rate by RBI to banks.
· Credit control: Expansionary policy (low rates, more money) during recession; Contractionary policy (high rates, less money) during inflation.
· Fiscal policy vs Monetary policy: Fiscal policy (government – taxation, spending) works alongside monetary policy (RBI – interest rates, money supply).

---

 2: Balance of Payments (BOP)

· Balance of payments: Systematic record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world during a specific period (usually one year).
· BOP components (must balance overall):
  · Current account: Records trade in goods and services, income (investment earnings, remittances), and current transfers.
    · Trade balance = Exports - Imports (Surplus if exports > imports; Deficit if imports > exports)
  · Capital account: Records capital transfers (debt forgiveness, migration of assets) and acquisition/disposal of non-produced assets.
  · Financial account (often combined with capital account): Records foreign investment (FDI, FII), loans, banking capital, reserves.
· Foreign exchange: BOP affects currency demand/supply – deficit puts pressure on currency depreciation.
· Economic indicators: Current Account Deficit (CAD) is closely monitored. Persistent large deficit indicates economic vulnerability.

---

 3: Unemployment

· Unemployment: People who are willing, able, and actively seeking work but cannot find employment.
· Types of unemployment:
  · Frictional: Temporary between jobs (healthy, shows labor mobility)
  · Structural: Mismatch between skills available and skills demanded (technology change, industry decline)
  · Cyclical: Due to economic recession/downturn (demand-deficient)
  · Seasonal: Occurs during certain seasons (agriculture, tourism)
  · Disguised: More people employed than needed (zero marginal productivity – common in agriculture)
  · Voluntary: Choosing not to work at prevailing wages
· Causes: Lack of skills, slow economic growth, population growth, labor laws, automation, seasonal factors.
· Consequences: Poverty, social unrest, lost output (GDP loss), skill degradation, mental health issues.
· Solutions: Skill development programs, employment generation schemes (MGNREGA in India), encouraging entrepreneurship, labor reforms, economic growth promotion.
· Labor market: Measured by unemployment rate (unemployed / labor force × 100).

---

 4: International Trade and Its Benefits

· International trade: Exchange of goods, services, and capital across international borders. Exports (selling abroad), Imports (buying from abroad).
· Comparative advantage: Countries should produce goods where they have lower opportunity cost and trade for others – increases global output and efficiency.
· Benefits of international trade:
  · Access to wider variety of goods/services
  · Lower prices due to competition and specialization
  · Larger markets for producers (economies of scale)
  · Technology and knowledge transfer
  · Increased employment in export sectors
  · Higher GDP and economic growth
· Trade barriers (restrictions): Tariffs (taxes on imports), quotas (quantity limits), subsidies to local producers, non-tariff barriers (standards, regulations).
· WTO (World Trade Organization) : International body that regulates trade rules, resolves disputes, promotes free trade through multilateral agreements.
· Economic integration levels (increasing integration): Free Trade Area → Customs Union → Common Market → Economic Union → Political Union (e.g., EU).

 1: Economic Liberalization and Its Impact

· Economic liberalization: Reforms that reduce government control, open markets to private participation, and integrate with global economy.

· Reforms (India 1991 as example): Deregulation, privatization of public sector, reduction in tariffs and trade barriers, tax reforms, allowing foreign direct investment (FDI).

· Globalization: Increased cross-border flow of goods, services, capital, technology, and labor resulting from liberalization.

· Economic growth (positive impacts): Higher GDP growth, increased productivity, more efficient resource allocation, rise of new industries (IT, telecom, services).

· Foreign investment: FDI and FII inflows bring capital, technology, management expertise, and global market access.

· Market opening: Domestic industries face competition, leading to better quality and lower prices for consumers.

· Economic transformation (challenges): Job displacement in traditional sectors (agriculture, small-scale industry), increased income inequality, vulnerability to global financial crises.

· Overall impact: Liberalized economies typically grow faster but require safety nets for affected populations.

---

2: Economic Inequality and Its Measures

· Economic inequality: Unequal distribution of income, wealth, and opportunities among individuals or groups within an economy.

· Gini coefficient: Most common measure – ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality). Higher Gini = greater inequality. Example: India ~0.35, South Africa ~0.63, Nordic countries ~0.25.

· Income distribution: Share of total national income received by different population segments (often studied as top 10% vs bottom 10% or quintiles).

· Poverty: Extreme form of inequality – lack of minimum income for basic needs. Measured by poverty line and poverty headcount ratio.

· Social justice: Unequal societies face issues of fairness, social cohesion, political instability, and unequal access to education/healthcare.

· Wealth gap: Often wider than income gap – includes assets (property, stocks, savings) passed across generations.

· Economic policies to reduce inequality: Progressive taxation (higher tax on rich), social welfare programs (subsidies, cash transfers), minimum wage laws, public investment in education and healthcare, land reforms.

 3: Sustainable Development

· Sustainable development: Development that meets present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

· Three pillars (must balance all):
  · Economic growth: Prosperity, employment, innovation, infrastructure
  
· Environmental protection: Resource conservation, pollution control, biodiversity, climate action
 
 · Social equity: Poverty reduction, health, education, gender equality, inclusive communities

· Long-term planning: Short-term profits cannot come at cost of long-term environmental degradation or social unrest.

· Green economy: Low-carbon, resource-efficient, socially inclusive economic model – renewable energy, circular economy (reduce-reuse-recycle), sustainable agriculture.

· Key challenges: Climate change, resource depletion (water, fossil fuels, minerals), deforestation, pollution, loss of biodiversity.

· UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) : 17 global goals including no poverty, zero hunger, clean energy, responsible consumption, climate action.

· Trade-offs: Industrial growth vs pollution; job creation vs environmental regulation – sustainable development seeks win-win solutions.

4: Behavioral Economics

· Behavioral economics: Field combining economics and psychology to study how psychological factors influence decision making – challenges traditional assumption that humans are perfectly rational.
· Traditional economics assumption: Rational actors with perfect information, stable preferences, maximizing self-interest.
· Psychological factors: Emotions, social norms, fairness, habits, framing, mental shortcuts (heuristics).
· Market anomalies: Situations where actual behavior deviates from rational predictions – stock market bubbles, overpricing, herding behavior.
· Cognitive biases (systematic errors in thinking):
  · Loss aversion: Fear of loss stronger than desire for gain (losing ₹100 hurts more than gaining ₹100 feels good)
  · Anchoring: Relying too heavily on first piece of information
  · Confirmation bias: Seeking evidence that confirms existing beliefs
  · Present bias: Overvaluing immediate rewards over future benefits
  · Overconfidence: Overestimating one's own abilities
· Economic prediction: Behavioral economics improves predictions by incorporating realistic human behavior – used in public policy ("nudge" units), marketing, finance (understanding irrational market movements).
· Applications: Designing better pension plans (opt-out vs opt-in), reducing energy consumption (social comparison feedback), improving health choices (default healthy options).
